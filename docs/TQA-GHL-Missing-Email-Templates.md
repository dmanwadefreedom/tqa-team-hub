Hey [First Name],

Quick thing I didn't have time to cover in the session.

When IT professionals start applying for cybersecurity roles, they almost always lead with their technical skills. Years of sysadmin experience. Network certs. Cloud platforms they've managed.

And they get passed over by candidates with less experience.

Here's why.

Cybersecurity hiring managers aren't looking for the best technician. They're looking for someone who thinks like a defender. Someone who sees a system and immediately asks: "Where would this break? What's the risk? What would I do first if something went wrong at 2 AM?"

That mindset is the filter. Not the certifications. Not the years of experience. The way you frame problems.

The good news: if you've spent years in IT, you already think this way. You just haven't learned to say it out loud in an interview yet.

Three things you can do this week — no program, no cost, no commitment:

1. Pick a system you manage at work. Write down the three biggest security risks it has. That exercise alone trains you to speak the language hiring managers listen for.

2. Read one breach report. Not the news article — the actual post-mortem. Mandiant and CrowdStrike publish these for free.

3. Find one cybersecurity professional on LinkedIn whose career path started in IT. Study how they describe their transition in their summary. Notice the language.

That's it. No pitch. Just something useful.

### 6. NURTURE-VALUE-2 (Day 7)
**Fires:** Day 7 of nurture sequence | **From:** DJ Naronikar

**Subject: 3 cybersecurity certifications that actually lead to jobs (and 2 that don't)**

Hey [First Name],

The certification question is the one I get asked most. So let me give you the straight answer.

**Three that matter — in this order:**

**1. CompTIA Security+ ($404 exam)**
Your entry ticket. HR departments use it as a checkbox. Without it, your resume doesn't make it past the first filter. Most people with an IT background can pass it with 4-6 weeks of focused study.

**2. CISSP ($749 exam)**
The one that changes your salary. CISSP holders earn 25% more on average. You need 5 years of experience in two or more security domains for full certification — but you can pass the exam first and work as an "Associate of ISC2" while you build hours.

**3. One specialization cert based on your target role:**
- Cloud security? CCSP or AWS Security Specialty.
- Penetration testing? CEH or OSCP.
- Governance and risk? CISM.
Pick one. Not three. One.

**Two that waste your money if you're transitioning:**

- **SANS/GIAC certs** — $8,000-$9,000 per course. Excellent content, but the ROI doesn't make sense until you're already in a cybersecurity role and your employer is paying.
- **Generic "cybersecurity bootcamp" certificates** — from platforms that charge $10K+ but have no employer recognition. Ask: "Can you show me 5 people who got hired because of this certificate?" If they can't, walk away.

The right certification in the right order saves you 6-12 months. The wrong one costs you the same.

Reply if you want help figuring out which path makes sense for your background.
